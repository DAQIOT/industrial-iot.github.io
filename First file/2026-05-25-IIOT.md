1. My Understanding of Industrial IoT (IIoT)

A common misconception is equating consumer IoT with Industrial IoT. In fact, they serve completely different scenarios with distinct technical standards and core requirements.

Consumer IoT focuses on convenience, entertainment and daily consumption scenarios.

Unlike consumer IoT that prioritizes user experience and entertainment, Industrial IoT demands extreme stability, high reliability, data security, low latency and long-term operability. Its ultimate goal is to help factories reduce operating costs and improve production efficiency through digital means.

Essentially, IIoT breaks the data isolation of traditional industrial equipment. It connects factory machines, testing instruments, sensors and control systems, realizing unified collection, stable transmission, intelligent analysis, real-time early warning and full data traceability of production and equipment data. This is the core foundation of smart manufacturing and industrial digital transformation.

A standard and mature Industrial IoT system adopts a four-layer architecture, covering all links from on-site perception to cloud application:

- Sensing Layer: On-site data collection terminals, including industrial infrared pyrometers, power meters, flow meters, temperature and vibration sensors, and all kinds of industrial detection instruments

- Network Layer: 4G/5G, LoRa, industrial Ethernet, RS485/RS232 serial buses, industrial switches

- Edge Layer: Core industrial gateways responsible for protocol conversion, data parsing, local data caching, offline resume transmission and on-site edge computing

- Cloud Platform Layer: Real-time data uploading, visual monitoring, abnormal alarm notification, equipment lifecycle management and industrial data statistical analysis

3. Why Do Many IIoT Projects Fail? (On-Site Summary)

After participating in dozens of industrial IoT projects, I have concluded that most project failures stem from neglecting harsh industrial field details rather than technical or equipment problems:

- Unreasonable equipment selection that fails to adapt to industrial harsh environments (high temperature, oil pollution, electromagnetic interference, mechanical vibration)

- Data acquisition anomalies caused by mismatched communication protocols, wrong baud rates or incorrect device address configuration

- Discontinuous data records due to the absence of local cache and offline data transmission functions under unstable network conditions

- Blind cloud access without considering on-site bandwidth constraints and overseas network latency issues

A qualified industrial IoT solution must be field-adaptive, highly stable and easy to maintain — practicability always comes first in industrial scenarios.
